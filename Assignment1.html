Introduction

Many major cities around the world have been enjoying the bicycle boom for the last decade. Some cities, such as Amsterdam and Copenhagen are known for their high bicycle mode share and prove that the perception of comfort or safety, two of the main commonly assumed drawbacks of bicycle, are subjective arguments and can be influenced by cultural thrust, policies involvement, health and environmental campaigns, etc.

Non-motorized modes in general are a key element for sustainable and inclusive transport systems, and was yet very popular during the 19th. century in Paris or London. The development of the car meanwhile the post wars economy and industrial development have shifted the society and the cities to a larger scale, by reducing the time-distances, and offering the city center's work and recreational areas to suburbans. This popularization of individual motorized vehicles caused several major changes, especially in both an urban planning, mobility and transportation system perspective. The development of the urban design method of zoning, initially used in Paris for bringing jobs into suburbs, and then imported in New York City as a result of the NIMBY protests — Not In My Back Yard) — (Harvey, 2008; Erickson, 2012), for finally being transformed as a massive top-down urban planning tool in Chinese cities along the 20th. century (Leroux, 2016; Ruzicka-Rossier, 2014), has transformed people into car-captive individuals, reducing their alternative-mode accessibility. Along with this car-oriented policies, the disappearance of tramways in most European cities has reduced the public transportation system to a poor alternative instead of developing it. This is also where the question of the "right to city" comes up (Costes, 2008; Harvey, 2008).

Nowadays, positive and negative thrusts lead city planners, stakeholders and people to reconsider the use of other modes than car. Former mayor of Bogotá, Enrique Peñalosa, use to explain that "An advanced city is not a place where the poor move about in cars, rather it’s where even the rich use public transportation" (TED talk, 2013). It avoids consistently the risks due to individual motorized vehicles driving errors, reduce the congestion in cities, promote the social diversity and usually they are modes powered by low-polluting energy. The public transportation development is critical for decongesting the city, and the active-transportation are critical to address and alleviate the "first and last mile problem" (Mineta Transportation Institute, 2009), insure equity in accessibility to mobility and developing the life between buildings (Jan Gehl, 2011 b). Thus mobility is not anymore a simple derived demand of activity, but also a social, inclusive and environmental vector.

The purpose of this research (Master's thesis) is to attempt integrating the Bicycle Travel Demand to a Land-Use and Transport (LUT) Model implemented on the commercial software Cube Land + Voyager (and encrypted in Python) for the Greater Boston Area (GBA), Massachusetts, USA. The LUT Boston is exploited in particular to investigate the effects of sea level rise on the city, in both land-use and transport perspective, and in a short and longer terms. The transportation-related models follow the 4-step model, which is calibrated on Travel surveys from 1990 and 2010. This includes 1.68 million households in the Greater Boston Area for a total population of 4.5 million divided onto 986 Traffic Analysis Zones (TAZs). The transportation network includes the layer of roads, as well as the transit network layer. Nevertheless, the bicycle network, including the bicycle path and bicycle lanes, are not integrated yet in the LUT model. For the purpose of this project, we will need to integrate it for modeling purposes. Indeed, the Urban Environment highly influences the cyclist in his decision-making process, and the bicycle travel demand, as well as the route choice, might be highly correlated to the Level of Service (LOS) of the chosen path.

Thus, the challenge lies in capturing and exploiting the interrelations between the urban environment, its perception and the cyclists. Barely documented yet, bicycle travel demand becomes more popular among the transportation and mobility researcher community particularly because of the installation of bike sharing systems in major cities around the world, and therefore the access to a wide range of data about bicycle flows.

The consideration of bicycle into a land-use and transport model implies to reconsider the mode choice models as well as the route choice models structuring the popular four-step model. The trip generation and distribution are independent of the chosen mode of transport. Thus, this first assignment aims at providing with the general knowledge about Transport Modeling, and give a reasonable direction to this project (in terms of timing constraints and knowledge to cover).

The document is structured as follows. First, the motivations of this work and how shall we get interested in the bicycle travel demand as transportation engineers and urban planners. Then a data analysis section present the exploration of some bicycle data we have been able to collect. Since those data sets are not complete in terms of trip information nor in terms of individual users' characteristics, the aim is to have qualitative feelings of bicycle trends in the Boston Urban Area, and thus to feed the modeling part as well as the modeler knowledge. This will yield a perception analysis approach of Urban Environment since it has a high influence on cycling. The last section, Bicycle Travel Demand Models, depict our modeling intervention in the LUT Boston models, and provide with a review of existing models as well as a first critic of each of these models.

Motivation

This research aims at stepping back on the LUT model structure by integrating the bicycle travel demand to the four-step model, as well as integrating the bicycle facilities and lanes into the network. The stakes of considering bicycle trips in a Land-use and Transport model are plural and motivated by the following.

The stakes of integrating bicycle travel demand

First, to consider the bicycle travel demand aims at considering (or stop ignoring) the policies that are being carried out by the GBA (Greater Boston Area), especially in Boston, Cambridge and Somerville, which promote the use of alternative modes — active modes — by building cycling facilities, giving priority to bicycle on the road, and slowly teaching drivers how to share the road. It has been demonstrated that these policies have a huge impact on people's behavior and have the strength to change people's habits. Since the GBA is promoting the bicycle, the amount of cyclist have considerably increased in the past 15 years, and should continue to increase since a certain critical mass of cyclists have been reached, which encourage municipalities to invest and promote cycling facilities.

Second, the land-use part of the model is interesting as well, and could be a source for further investigations. Indeed, the mode choice might be highly correlated to the plan of the city, in terms of distance to travel to reach the destination. The success of car in the 20th. century has broadened cities and shortened the time distances. The consequences on urban planning has made people car-captive, particularly in the US. In the mean time, the over-density of city centers paired with the increasing congestion due to increasing urban population tends toward a saturation point in this car-oriented society. In addition, planners are getting aware of the quality of proximity in cities, that catalyze lively neighborhoods and yields equity in the access to mobility. The (re-)development of the mixed-use planning and the appropriation of old industrial areas could address the car-captive phenomena. If we think a future with a higher proximity and diversity, then the geographical distance would decrease again, and low the interest of choosing the mode "car", because it is less suitable for short-trips. Thus, we have a lot of reasons to imagine the future of cities with a lower rate of car expansion, or even a future with car-free neighborhoods. The rising awareness on environmental issues is also a driving force to imagine future of cities with less cars. Integrating the bicycle travel demand in the land-use and transport model could produce innovative land affectations and enable the model to consider this circle of "proximity calls active modes, and active modes call proximity".

Finally, it might be interesting to extend and explore the sea level rise scenario with the transport mode "bicycle", which could be a good alternative in case of a network disruption on a short-term basis because of its flexibility and affordability qualities.

Bicycle Policies in Cambridge

As mentioned before, the development of the car-oriented society lead to city sprawl, enabled urban planning through zoning, and made people car-captive for some trips. Rates of car-ownership and car-use have increased sharply over the past few decades, while rates of walking and cycling have fallen (Banister (2005); IRF (2007)). But the rising trend of cycling is changing the habits of people and deciders. The willingness to develop cleaner modes is well illustrated by the wide development of bike sharing systems around major cities, and its success in most of them. More specifically, inside the GBA, the city of Cambridge invests a lot of efforts in the active-transportation modes development.

The Cambridge bicycle network plan (more info here) releases some data as well as data analysis of the bicycle state on the city. The two bar charts below show the fast growing of bicycle use rate in the city. On the left figure, they aggregated the data obtained from all the counting spots of the city, and on the right figure, they show the data of the specific intersection of Massachusetts Av. and Vassar Street.

These two graphs are interesting in several ways. On the left, the absolute count of bicycle raised from 3000 cyclists in 2002 to more than 10.000 in 2012 and decreased again to 9.000 in 2014. This is explained, partially, by the fact that a lot of road works happened at the intersection where the count used to occur. On the right, the amount of cyclists almost doubled in four years. This is mostly explained by the construction, between 2010 and 2014, of a bicycle track on Vassar Street, with very convenient conditions to ride. Thus, despite the overall decrease in cyclist in Cambridge, this intersection base the importance of the urban environment for cyclists, as well as the willingness to develop this mode in the GBA.

Cycling in the Urban Environment

Active transportation modes are key element for sustainable transport system. With low emissions (for both noise and pollutants), and an accident risk factor much lower than cars, the use of non-motorized modes could contribute in having a more attractive urban environment, people-oriented rather than car-oriented. Furthermore, the impacts of active-transportation are going further since it is recognized and reported that cycling has a positive impact on the health problems and contribute to make a healthier city. Its provides direct benefits to the traveler, and indirect benefits to the society Rietveld et al. (2004), Pucher et al. (2010). But this section tries to tackle the relation between the urban environment and cyclists in the other way around: what are the impact of the urban environment on the bicycle travel demand? Broach et al. (2012) reviewed a bunch of studies that found a positive correlation between the global level of bicycling and density of the network of bicycle facility. At the disaggregated level, a strong correlation between cycling and proximity to separated paths have been reported, or that the cyclists go out of their way to use paths. There is also some evidence that gender and level of experience in cycling affect the perception of the network and traffic environment.

These correlations show that (1) the perception is subjective and depend on individual characteristics, and (2) that the policies promoting a good level of service for cyclists on the roads could increase the share of bicycle while decreasing the accident risks.

Cyclists Perceptions

The cyclist perceptions of a given mode, on a given route, at a given time, are complicated to comprehend because all those parameters are subjective to any individual (level of comfort, value of time, safety, environmental impact...). While riding a bicycle, the feeling of safety is essential and we can easily assume that it highly depends on the number of lanes, the speed limitation of car, if the bicycle track is a bicycle lane or a path (i.e., on- or off- the road), etc. As an example of the subjectivity of the perceptions, Baker (2009) suggested that women are an “indicator species” for cycling, saying that where women cycle, cycling is safe and convenient for everyone and thus suitable for an increase in the travel demand. It also means that, "despite our hope that gender roles do not exist" (quote by Jennifer Dill), it could definitely be a behavioral factor in the bicycle mode choice model.

Factors affecting cycling

Pucher et al. (1999) attempted to draw an exhaustive list of factors affecting cycling:

  • Public attitude and public image
  • City size and density
  • Cost of car use and public transport
  • Income
  • Climate (daily weather)
  • Danger
  • Cycling infrastructure

Among them, three are particularly interesting for modeling purposes. The income plays an important role, it influences the car ownership as well as the value of time of individuals. Shall we precise that considering that people with high income are more likely to drive does not mean that low incomes cycle because they do not have enough money for a "good" transportation mode? The bicycle mode is not anymore a "back-up mode", but often a choice of life-style. The second is bicycle infrastructure. It is hard to measure the impact on comfort and safety a bicycle lane can have, but Landis et al. (2014) investigated this feature by testing 150 participants on a 27 km route in Tampa, Florida. The route was composed of 30 roads segments with different conditions (traffic, number of lanes...). He finally ended-up with the Bicycle Level Of Service, detailed below. The third factor is the city size and density, also developed with further information below. We might be aware of transferability issues of the BLOS. Indeed, what is considered to be an insurmountable constraint in some cities, for a specific culture, might be considered as normal in others. Topography and weather are the best example since cold and hilly cities from Europe (Switzerland, Austria) have a higher cyclist rate than in flat and warmer cities of Southern Europe (Pucher, 2010). Actually, some studies show that sometimes those controverted factors do show a consequent effect on bicycle use, and sometimes they do not (Heinen et al., 2001). The unmeasurable thrust of public attitude, culture and habits might then be primordial in defining the potential of bicycle development in a city. Primordial but not irrevocable as several US cities have been able to extend the bike share rate by promoting active modes and making bicycle attractive. Portland, Oregon, and Minneapolis, Minnesota used to be car-oriented sprawled cities and succeeded in rising the share of bicycle from the late nineties to 2008 (from Bike Walk Twin Cities report (2008) and The Portland bicycle plan for 2030 (2010)).

To complete the former list, Rietveld et al. (2004) consider also the topography of the city, generalize the comparison to other mode through the concept of "attractiveness of competing modes", and the physical condition of the cyclist (comprising the age, disabilities...).

Finally, one last aspect could be the safety. Not only the safety related to road danger and car crashes, but the one of people riding a bike alone during the night, especially women, that can be an issue in certain cities.

Bicycle Level of Service (BLOS)

Usually used in the field of traffic engineering, the LOS skims (matrix of LOS vectors for each O/D pairs) are generated by estimating several attributes, such as the out- and in-vehicle times, the waiting time, the number of transfers, the fares and tolls, etc.

The Bicycle LOS engages more detailed factors, and is not completely established yet as much as it is for the traffic. The main and most cited papers exploring the bicycle level of service so far are the works of Landis et al. (1994), Axhausen et al. (1986), Epperson (1994), Davis (1987), Sorton (1994).

As introduced before, Landis et al. (2014) did more recently an experiment on 150 cyclists riding a 27 km route with different road segments. The experiment, which tested gender, level of experience of the cyclist, bicycle path characteristics and pavement conditions, yielded the following BLOS model structure:

\[ BLOS = f\big(V, S, M, X, P, W \big) = b + a_1\big[f(V)\big] + a_2\big[f(S)\big] + a_3\big[f(M)\big] + a_4\big[f(X)\big] + a_5\big[f(P)\big] + a_6\big[f(W)\big] \]

with

  • \(V\) the per-lane motor vehicle traffic volume;
  • \(S\) speed of motor vehicle;
  • \(M\) traffic mix;
  • \(X\) potential cross-traffic generation;
  • \(P\) pavement surface condition
  • \(W\) width for bicycling.

For the development of the BLOS model (choice and calibration of variables), Landis et al. (2014) produced the general form of the Bicycle LOS (BLOS) as a function of set of variables. Finally, the selected model was:

\[ BLOS = a_1 ln(Vol_{15}/L)+ a_2 ln[SPD_p(1+ \%HV)] + a_3 ln(COM_{15} \cdot NCA) + a_4(PC_5)^{−2} + a_5(W_e)^2 + C \]

Where (estimated parameters and the LOS category table are available in Landis et al. (2014)):

  • \(BLOS\) = perceived hazard of the shared-roadway environment,
  • \(Vol_{15}\) = volume of directional traffic in 15-min time period,
  • \(L\) = total number of through lanes,
  • \(SPD_p\) = posted speed limit (a surrogate for average running speed),
  • \(HV\) = percentage of heavy vehicles (as defined in the HCM),
  • \(COM_{15}\) = trip generation intensity of the land use adjoining the road segment,
  • \(NCA\) = effective frequency per mile of noncontrolled vehicular access (e.g., driveways and on-street parking spaces),
  • \(PC_5\) = 5-point pavement surface condition rating, and
  • \(W_e\) = average effective width of lane
  • \(C\) = constant

Note that the LOS should also correspond to a given trip purpose, and is perceived differently for each mode (in-vehicle time in a car or a train is not the same, etc).

The role of trip distance

The trip distance plays a more important role when it comes to human-powered transportation, and a fortiori the role of urban planning (land-use pattern and density) is critical as well. According to the NTPS, 48% of the trips in US cities are less than 3 miles (or 4.8 km) long, with 28% even shorter than 1 mile. In addition, it has been reported that in European countries, the distances to travel within a city are generally shorter than in the US (Ewing et al. 2001; Banister 2005; Newman et al. 1999), which could explain partially the higher rate of cyclists in Europe than in the US. To explain this phenomenon, the urbanistic patterns can help: the more the city is dense, compact, more it is amenable to cycle since a higher amount of destinations are accessible within a short-distance trip Pucher et al. (1999). Up to now, two main urban planning tools have been broadly used: mixed-use and zoning. With a mixed-use oriented city, the neighborhood are more compact and people can find all they need within a walking/riding distance. The mixed-use is generally more adapted to active-transportation modes and a good catalyst to sustainable communities (Duany et al. 2009; Gehl et al. 2001; Van der Ryn and Calthorpe 2008). With a zoning-oriented city, each zone of the city has a specific function and requires people to travel on average longer distances to realize the same sequence of activity, especially for the suburbans who work in city centers.

An other interesting element regarding the role of trip distance concerns the Value Of Time (VOT) of the cyclists. Indeed, except from the socioeconomic characteristics that influence the VOT, as well as the trip purpose, the duration of the trip is important. Jovicic et al. (2003) reported the VOT used in the Travel Demand Model of Copenhagen, based on a survey from 1992:

Commuter (HW) Leisure (PR) Education (HE) Business (BS)
Bus in-vehicle time 38.1 28.3 10.2 85.7

We see here that the bicycle time over 30 minutes, which correspond to approximatively 5 miles in urban area (according to GoogleMaps estimation for a trip in Cambridge), is very expensive, even more than twice the price of driving on a congested road.

Last, according to the study of cyclists by Broach et al. (2012), the average trip distance was 2.2 miles (3.5 km) for non-commute trips and 3.7 miles (6 km) for commute trips, for an average speed of 10 miles/h (16.1 km/h)). Among all the trips, around half of the recorded miles were ridden on facilities with bicycle infrastructure.

Discussion and recommendations

We end-up with a well documented but still complicated relationship between cyclists and the built environment. Complicated because it requires a multidisciplinary approach, including sociology (for public attitudes and cultural patterns), psychology (for understanding cyclists' behavior), urbanism and transportation engineering skills (to model). The diversity of cycling conditions in cities with a high mode share for bicycle as well as the human factor that yields subjectivity overall does not help.

Nevertheless, we can identify several sources of improvement to increase the number of cyclists in cities. The main thrust comes from the policies that have the power to catalyze public attitudes as well as changing comfort habits of people. The policies actions can have several forms, such as financial pressure (make car-ownership less affordable, tolls for individual motorized vehicle in-bound) as it happens in London to promote transit; making the car less efficient in urban areas (reducing car speed limitation to the average speed of bicycle, limit the number of lanes available for cyclists, as it happens in Copenhagen; offer a good linkage between transportation modes, as in happens in Boston (carry the bicycle on the bus or in the metro, park and ride facilities); advertise wellness; improving bicycle facilities and facilitating cyclist ride (public stations to repair the bike and inflate tires, separating transit lanes from cycle lanes, secure turn-right on red, allow to ride a one-way roads in both directions, broad bicycle parking spots...). For more ideas, Pucher et al. (2010) reported the policies to promote safe walking and cycling used in a few European countries.

Most of these actions go in the opposite direction of what traffic engineering have been doing in the 20th. century. But a lot of congestion problems could be dismissed by constraining the car use and by promoting both active transportation and public transportations. Martin Wachs (1998), Gordon et al. (1998) and Gehl in 2011 in several press articles, consider that "bicycle traffic continues to be not much more than political sweet talk" and that in most cities, no really effective measures have been undertaken so far to promote cycling, but easy and non-conflicting (with cars) measures. Therefore, it bases the motivation of this work to develop research around the bicycle field since it is called to increase considerably in the next years in cities all around the world.

We also have some information about Cambridge bicycle count over the past few years. The purpose is to explore and try to extract trends from those data to get a feeling of what influences the bicycle travel demand. This feeling might be useful to develop the models.

Bicycle Travel Demand

The bicycle travel demand modeling process is challenging and requires minor changes at different stages of the LUT model. This section depicts, reviews and proposes a non-exhaustive list of the different possible actions we will have to undertake to achieve integrating completely the bicycle travel demand. For the modeling part, even though the field of discrete choice modeling has been broadly applied to different models, the common denominator remains the Discrete choice methods developed by Ben-Akiva M. and Bierlaire M. (1999), particularly to correct the IIA problem (Independence of Irrelevant Alternatives) we will develop below, and the use of Biogeme package for parameters estimation (Bierlaire M., 2003)

Integrating the bicycle network layer

Since the urban environment plays a critical role in the bicycle travel demand as well as in the BLOS, it is essential to integrate the bicycle network layer into Cube. The Massachusetts Government made available a file of the bicycle trails GIS data and the City of Cambridge made available a lot of GIS information about the bicycle infrastructure. We still need to find a way to get the data for the rest of the GBA.

Forecasting Model of Bicycle use

In addition, we need to be able to estimate the growth rate of the bicycle share: how many people are expected to ride in 15 years, relatively to other modes ? If policies are promoting active modes, then how the car mode share will decrease? Also, should we consider that the bicycle ownership model does not exist and assume that everybody can access to a bicycle?

Mode Choice Model

The challenge in integrating the bicycle in a mode choice model lies in defining the skim of the generalized cost \( C^k_{ijm}\). What attributes should we consider to determine the cost of a bicycle trip, how do we take into account the daily variation of weather, as well as the variations in the activity sequence of the cyclist.

Identify the classes of people inclined to ride

The very first step of the mode choice modeling process will be to identify classes of people inclined to ride a bike. Usually, we consider different class of people, such as public transport (PT) captive (users that do not have access to a car), car-captive (users for who PT or active mobility are not realistic alternative) and free-travelers.

Explaining variables of Mode Choice

The explaining variables of the mode choice model must consider a wide range of individual and urban environment characteristics:

  • user's characteristics (income, car ownership, age, gender, activity, household structure, other activities)
  • trip characteristics (purpose, length, time period, season, number of people to carry together)
  • transport supply characteristics (trip duration, cost, built environment)
  • Other (weather, ecological involvement, comfort zone, reliability, safety, life style, ... very subjective!)

Model structure of Mode Choice

The model structure of the mode choice will probably an aggregated model based on the Multinomial Logit Model. For the moment, the LUT mode choice model considers only the driving, walking, and transit modes as alternatives, with specificities for occupancy vehicle rate and park and ride trips. The bicycle mode could be either nested to another category of modes, for instance we could cluster the "active modes" gathering walking and cycling. Nevertheless, the bicycle is different enough from walking and driving (e.g., in terms of trip length potential, or comfort attributes, etc) to be considered as an "elementary" mode.

The Multinomial Models (MNL) is widely used in discrete choice modeling and takes the following form:

\[ p_{m|ij} = \frac{exp(-\lambda^kC^k_{ijm})}{\sum_{r \in \{m\}}exp(-\lambda^kC^k_{ijr})}\]

Returning the probability of mode \(m\) to be chosen among the choice set of modes \({m}\), with \(i,j\) the origin and destination, \(k\) the segmentation of the population.

Consult the Travel Estimation Techniques for Urban Planning for further information and get the idea of the kind of parameters to consider in the utility functions.

Route choice Model

We want to fulfill two very specific requirements with our route choice model. The first one is to obtain a significant and consistent choice-set of routes to simplify the choice modeling steps by identifying the "non-chosen" routes. The second is to be able to consider, at some point, a measure of the perception of the urban environment. To do so, we can have two distinct approaches: either aggregate the level of service to the TAZs and have a single level of service on all the links of the TAZs, assuming that the neighborhood as a higher or lower propensity to make cyclist comfortable; or to add a Bicycle LOS variable to each link of the network.

In large networks, the set of path is usually unknown and the choice-set generation must extract good options from the network. Prato et al. (2007), demonstrated that the choices set highly influences the quality of the route choice model. Therefore several methods have been proposed to define a consistent choice-set, either with deterministic or stochastic models. The Path Enumeration Algorithms are used for this purpose and different heuristics have been proposed:

Also called repeated shortest path. No, mainly based on the travel time. Might be improved with labeling method.

The stochastic route choice-set generation by Bovy et al. (2007) has been widely used as a foundation of further works. Also known as the doubly stochastic choice set generation model and seems to be the best option for obtaining a route choice-set for bicycle (ie. that can consider the impact of the built environment on the route choice). Only three major studies (Schüssler and Axhausen (2009), Broach et al. (2012) and Hood et al. (2013)) exist on the route choice modeling specifically for bicycle as far as I know. The methods are reported in the table below:

Authors Data Description Model
Schüssler and Axhausen (2009) GPS data, 2435 cyclists, Zürich. Only coordinates. First route choice model for bicyclists, estimated from a large sample of GPS observations and overcomes the limitations inherent in the generally employed stated preference approach. link elimination approach, generated using the infrastructure provided the multi-agent transport simulation toolkit (MATSim)
Broach et al. (2012) GPS data, 164 non-recreational cyclists, Portland (Oregon). Only coordinates. Two steps method: (1) generation of multiple optima by varying the label cost function parameters, and (2) range over the which the parameter varies is calibrated with observed distribution of shortest path deviations. modification of labeling (Broach et al., 2010)
Hood et al. (2013) GPS data, 366 cyclists, San Fransisco. Coordinates + individual characteristics. Both link attributes and generalized cost coefficients are randomized for each shortest path search, and the coefficients of the distribution are extracted from the network. Yielded from doubly stochastic model (Bovy and Fiorenzo-Catalano, 2007)

Remark 1

Bovy et al. (2007) recommend two options for defining the parameters of the random distribution:

  • choose a distribution that exists in the literature with satisfying results and accept the bias of transferability
  • Lead a data-driven optimization, based on the proportions of the observations reproduced.

Remark 2

Broach et al. (2012 reported that where alternate paths share common links, they also presumably have correlated error components. This violates the multinomial logit (MNL) model assumption of independently distributed errors across alternatives. According to Frejinger and Bierlaire (2009), a correction factor can be applied to partially adjust the utilities for overlap. They used the PSL (Path-size Logit) approach, retaining the underlying MNL structure, such that:

\[ PS_{in} = \sum_{a \in \Gamma_i} \frac{l_a}{L_i} \cdot \frac{1}{\sum_{j \in C_n} \big(\frac{L_i}{L_j} \big)^\gamma \delta_{aj}} \]

Remark 3

Studying the route choice requires a lot of data regarding the actual route people are more likely to choose for their ride. Since we do not have any data to estimate a route choice model for GBA, and understand the decision-making of cyclists, we will have to find a way to yield an acceptable route choice model. We could, for instance, use GooogleMaps bicycle proposed routes (requesting from GoogleMaps API). It could be also a modified shortest path algorithm that takes into account the BLOS and therefore the built environment.
